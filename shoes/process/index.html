Process

Making “Shoes”

After figuring out just what the hell EGG is, we moved on to an internal dialogue that ran something like this:

Awesome, we thought. We are so awesome.

Um… now what? What topic should we focus on for our first pilot project? The environment? Poverty? Education? Nutrition? International issues? National issues? Local issues? The list goes on and on…

And really, who are we to discuss any of these topics in the first place?

But we know one thing: we can make visuals — beautiful visuals. And we can tell a story…

So we started there.

Instead of focusing on one issue, we decided to touch on them all by starting a discussion on aid. But “aid” is a big topic (duh). So we boiled the story down to one main point:

Giving is good. Giving money is best.

But, as with anything that’s been simplified to one core thought, there’s a much larger idea — in fact, many ideas. These ideas aren’t ours. These ideas come from people who have dedicated their lives and careers to figuring out how those of us with resources can best help those of us without.

Read our full write-up about the thinking behind “Shoes.”

Getting Started

When it comes to making stuff, we know what we’re doing. We’ve been doing it for over a decade. Sharing our process with the EGGsters was a new experience, though. Everyone involved — the mentors and the mentees — learned a ton.

While we followed the same process Psyop employs for commercial production, we allowed for inspiration and improvisation along the way.

The Song

In creating the brief for “Shoes,” we eventually settled on doing a tongue-in-cheek music video, something in the spirit of Psyop’s “Anthem”. Fun but subversive.

The only trouble was that we needed a song. So Psyop’s Justin Cone gave it a shot. He set up his keyboard and microphone in his cramped apartment, had a couple beers and recorded a draft.

Q Department to the Rescue

We knew we needed the song to be really special. It needed to be the engine for all the visuals. So we were ecstatic when long-time friend Q Department agreed to help.

They took the rough draft of the track and whipped it into shape. It went through a few iterations throughout the production of “Shoes,” eventually sounding like everything else Q Department does: awesome.

The Look

Character Design

Before we could tell the story of “Shoes,” we had to figure out what its characters would look like. The success of an animation hinges upon character design. They need to be relatable, flexible and fun.




Style Frames

Around the same time we developed storyboards, we created style frames. Style frames indicate the overall look of a project. Ideally, style frames look like stills from the final project — before the final project has even been created.

Style frames function as reference throughout a production, so it’s important to get them right.




Animation

Notice how much work we’ve done before even animating a single frame? Without all the pre-production work above, the animation could have easily gone off the rails.

Animation is an iterative process. We start rough and add more and more polish, making adjustments along the way.

Board-o-matic

The first step is to put the storyboards on a timeline with music. We call this a board-o-matic.

Previz

Previsualization is the process of roughing in characters, environments and animation. It’s meant to be quick and dirty while conveying enough detail to make adjustments to timing and the overall narrative.

The Dance

The opening scene of “Shoes” features a fairly complicated dance sequence. In order to pull that off, we needed some reference video. So EGGster Susan Yung choreographed a number for the occasion.

Final Product

After many, many more tweaks and iterations, we put the whole thing in the oven, grabbed a beer and — voila, out pops the final animation. More or less.
