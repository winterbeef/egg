Why Not Shoes?

Your old stuff vs. cash

So here’s the dirty secret. Nobody actually wants the old stuff you donate. Ouch. I know, the truth hurts.

It gets harsher: the one thing that you CAN donate and know it’ll help? Money.

Hear us out.

Disaster strikes, twice

On Tuesday, January 12, 2010 a 7.0 earthquake struck Port-au-Prince, Haiti. 3 million people were affected — 316,000 died, 300,000 were injured and 1,000,000 were left homeless.

Haiti earthquake: Red Cross Red Crescent response / Photo by Noora Kero/IFRC p-HTI0309

Immediately afterwards, Red Cross spent days explaining not only that they didn’t want, but couldn’t take donated items.

Claire Durham from the Red Cross international logistics team finally wrote a blog entry entitled, “Help not hinder Haiti.” She writes:

Relieving suffering should be guided solely by need and not what people have to donate. Humanitarian aid should also ‘do no harm.’ Quite a lot of harm is done when unwanted and unneeded fresh food items rot in piles at the airports and seaports, stopping medicines and blankets getting through.

A disaster is already chaotic, Durham explains. Trucks and boats full of donated items just add to the chaos.

A disaster is already chaotic. Getting boatloads of donated items just add to the chaos.

Saundra Schimmelpfennig writes goodintents.org, a blog dedicated to educating donors on how to effectively give. Donated ‘stuff’ really is:

Trash… in a box.

After the 1998 Hurricane Mitch in Honduras, shipping containers full of high heels and skeleton-shaped lollipops were received, when what they really needed medical supplies.

Congo has received broken bicycles and used toothbrushes.

Donations can kill.

Like donated medicines. With instructions in different languages, different dosages and different expiration dates, overdosing or underdosing is a real risk.

Most Dangerous? Baby formula. Formula requires clean water. Guess what’s really hard to find after a disaster or in areas suffering from extreme poverty? Clean water. Contaminated water causes diarrhea, one of the top global killers of children under the age of five.

Shipping.

Shipping costs money that could be spent on, well, anything else.

Sorting.

Ok, sure a volunteer could do it, but it’s more cost efficient for that person to work an extra hour at their own higher-paying job and donate the money from that hour.

Storage.

In a trauma-stricken country, space is already hard to come by. After the 1998 Hurricane Mitch in Honduras, ports were clogged with donated stuff, stopping delivery of goods like medical supplies that were actually needed.

Hurts local businesses.

In 1988, Nigeria’s textile sector employed over 400,000 people, second only to government administration jobs. Layi Adeloye, a reporter for The Punch, a Nigerian newspaper wrote that by 2006, the textile industry had collapsed, taking with it over half a million jobs.

According to Garth Frazer, an economist from the University of Toronto, used clothing imports explain “40% of the decline in African apparel production and roughly 50% of the decline in apparel employment” from 1981 to 2000.

Hurricane Sandy

Boat On the Tracks near MNR’s Ossining Station / Photo by MTAPhotos

Hurricane Sandy is interesting. Our offices were shuttered after Sandy, and many of us employees felt the urge that everyone else had: What can we do?

We donated stuff, we donated time, we donated money. The difference is that all donations were targeted to very specific and frequently updated needs. Like many in the area, we knew people who had lost everything. They made requests (diapers, blankets, heavy duty trash bags), and we tried to fill the requests.

FEMA posted some useful guidelines for volunteering and donating targeted specifically at Sandy cleanup efforts.

Despite that, just a couple days after Hurricane Sandy made landfall, Mayor Bloomberg said the city had been inundated with goods and volunteers.

Guess what he asked for? Money.

Does it Work?

There’s good news. Individual donors in the US give over 70 times more than large corporations, foundations and bequests every year.

But what if the charities aren’t even helping? That’s a lot of wasted cashola. Economists Dean Karlan and Jacob Appel write in their book More Than Good Intentions: “…if enough small donors start to reward aid organizations for providing credible demonstrations of their impacts, you can bet that better programs will ultimately result.”

And it’s easy to do. We just need to stop giving money to the programs that don’t work and give more money to the programs that do.

Last year, a couple of books came out that dig into the question of exactly how to evaluate aid. We already mentioned one, More Than Good Intentions by Dean Karlan and Jacob Appel. The other is Poor Economics: A Radical Rethinking of the Way to Fight Global Poverty by Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo.
